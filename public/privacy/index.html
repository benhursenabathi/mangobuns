Switchy

Privacy Policy

Last updated: July 10, 2026

The short version

Switching is fully local — your Macs talk directly to each other and no switching data ever leaves your devices. The only external connections Switchy makes are license activation and periodic validation with Lemon Squeezy, and software update checks. There are no accounts, no analytics, and no tracking.

What Switchy does

Switchy is a macOS menu bar app that switches Apple Magic accessories (Keyboard, Trackpad, Mouse) between Macs on your local network. All communication happens directly between your Macs over your local network using Bonjour discovery and Bluetooth.

Data we collect

We do not collect analytics, usage data, or personal profiles. Device switching runs entirely on your Mac and your local network. The only data that leaves your device is what is needed to activate and validate your license (see below) and standard software update checks.

Licensing and trial

New installations include a 3-day free trial that runs entirely on your Mac — starting it requires no account and sends nothing to us. When you purchase and activate a license, Switchy sends your license key and a device identifier to Lemon Squeezy's License API to activate that Mac (each license covers up to 5 Macs; you can deactivate a Mac at any time from Settings). Your license state is stored securely in the macOS Keychain on your device. Switchy periodically revalidates the license with Lemon Squeezy; temporary network failures do not lock you out.

Third-party services

Switchy does not include any analytics, crash reporting, or tracking frameworks. The only third-party service the app communicates with is Lemon Squeezy, for license activation and validation as described above.

Purchases

Purchases are processed by LemonSqueezy, our payment provider. LemonSqueezy handles all payment information. We do not have access to your full payment details. Please refer to LemonSqueezy's privacy policy for details on how they handle payment data.

Software updates

Switchy checks for updates via a publicly hosted appcast file. This check sends a standard HTTPS request that may include your IP address in server logs, as with any web request. No personal information is included in these requests.
