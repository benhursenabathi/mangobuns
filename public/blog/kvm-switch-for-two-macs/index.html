Do You Need a KVM Switch for Two Macs?

July 2026 · 5 min read

You have two Macs and one good set of peripherals. The classic answer from the PC world is a KVM switch — a box that shares one Keyboard, Video (monitor), and Mouse between computers at the push of a button.

For Macs with Apple's Magic accessories, that classic answer mostly doesn't work. Here's why, and what to use instead.

The problem: Magic devices are Bluetooth

A KVM switch physically routes wired USB signals from your keyboard and mouse to whichever computer is selected. Your Magic Keyboard, Magic Trackpad, and Magic Mouse don't have that cable — they pair to a Mac over Bluetooth. There's nothing for the KVM to switch.

Can you just plug them in? Partially:

  • Magic Keyboard: works as a wired keyboard while plugged in via USB-C or Lightning — so it can go through a KVM, but you've turned your wireless keyboard into a tethered one.
  • Magic Trackpad: same story — works wired, but tethered.
  • Magic Mouse: doesn't work at all. Its charging port is on the underside, so it physically can't be used while plugged in. No KVM can help.

Short version: a hardware KVM defeats the point of Apple's wireless peripherals — and the Magic Mouse can't participate, period.

What a KVM is still good for

Hardware KVMs earn their price (decent USB-C/Thunderbolt models run $60–$300+) in three situations:

  1. One monitor, two machines. The "V" in KVM. If both Macs share a single display, a KVM (or just your monitor's input switcher plus a software solution for input) is the way.
  2. Mixed platforms. A Mac and a Windows PC or Linux box — software input-sharing across platforms exists (Input Leap, Synergy) but is fiddlier than a hardware box.
  3. Wired peripherals. If you use a wired mechanical keyboard and a wired mouse anyway, a KVM works exactly as designed.

The software alternatives for two Macs

If each Mac has its own screen — the common MacBook + desktop setup — you don't need video switching at all. You just need your input devices to follow you. Three options:

1. Universal Control (free, same Apple ID)

Apple's built-in feature forwards your keyboard and cursor to a second Mac or iPad when you push the cursor off the screen edge. Great for side-by-side desks, but both Macs must be awake, nearby, and signed in to the same Apple Account — which rules out most work + personal combinations. More in our Universal Control deep-dive.

2. Input Leap / Synergy (free–$$, cross-platform)

Open-source (Input Leap) and commercial (Synergy) "software KVMs" that share one keyboard and mouse across computers over the network — including Windows and Linux. Powerful, but they require a server/client setup, both machines awake, and the peripherals stay attached to the host machine.

3. Switch the Bluetooth pairing itself (Switchy)

Switchy takes the direct route: one click in the menu bar and your Magic Keyboard, Trackpad, and Mouse disconnect from this Mac and pair to the other one — as if you'd done the System Settings dance, minus the dance. The devices genuinely belong to the target Mac, so everything works at native speed, with different Apple IDs, even when the first Mac goes to sleep. Bottom line

Buy a KVM if you're sharing one monitor or mixing a Mac with a PC. Use software if both Macs have screens and you love your Magic accessories — Universal Control when everything lives on one desk and one Apple ID, Switchy when you want your devices to actually move between Macs.
